Vincent Van Gogh (1853-1890)
A Turbulent Life of Artistic Genius

Vincent van Gogh (1853-1890) was a Dutch post-impressionist artist, renowned for his bold, expressive,

and emotionally charged paintings. Despite struggling with mental illness and poverty throughout his life,

Van Gogh produced an incredible body of work, leaving an indelible mark on the art world.

Early Life and Artistic Beginnings

Born in Groot-Zundert, Netherlands, Van Gogh was the eldest son of a Protestant pastor.

He was close to his younger brother Theo, who would later become his confidant and supporter.

Van Gogh's early life was marked by instability and uncertainty, with multiple career changes and relocations.


In 1880, Van Gogh decided to pursue a career in art, despite having no formal training.

He moved to Brussels to study art, but his early work was met with rejection and criticism.

Undeterred, he continued to develop his skills, experimenting with different techniques and mediums.

Artistic Style and Influences

Expressive brushwork: Thick, heavy brushstrokes that conveyed emotion and energy.

Vibrant color palette: Bold, clashing colors that added to the emotional intensity of his paintings.

Distorted forms: Elongated, exaggerated shapes that gave his paintings a sense of tension and dynamism.


Van Gogh's work was influenced by various artists and movements, including:

Impressionism : The emphasis on capturing light and color.

Japanese ukiyo-e prints : The use of bold, flat colors and compositional simplicity

Paul Gauguin : A fellow post-impressionist artist who encouraged Van Gogh to experiment with new techniques.
